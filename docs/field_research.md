# Field Research Documentation
**Project:** AfriMarket – Local Marketplace App
**Research Date:** Week 1
**Location:** Kigali, Rwanda (Kimironko Market, Nyabugogo, Remera)

---

## Research Methodology

Each team member conducted independent field observation (10-15 minutes) followed by a one-on-one user interview. Observations were done at local markets, roadside stalls, and residential neighborhoods. Notes were taken manually on-site and reviewed immediately after each session.

---

## Member 1 – Field Observations (Kimironko Market, 9:30–9:45 AM)

**Setting:** Main open-air market, fruit/vegetable section and second-hand clothing area.

**Timestamped Observations:**
- **09:31** – A woman moves between three stalls comparing tomato prices verbally. No price tags visible anywhere.
- **09:33** – A vendor shares photos of his stock via personal WhatsApp: *"Nimureba hano, nzakurekura igiciro"* ("Look at this, I'll give you a good price").
- **09:36** – A customer asks about MoMo payment. Confusion on both sides about how much to request.
- **09:39** – A woman passes four avocado stalls but cannot assess quality from a distance and keeps walking.
- **09:43** – A new customer asks for a seller of modern clothes. Two bystanders point in opposite directions.

**Friction Points:**
1. No price displays — buyers negotiate at every stall, making comparison impossible.
2. Product discovery requires physical walking — no way to find an item without searching the whole market.
3. New buyers cannot assess seller trustworthiness before approaching.
4. MoMo payment steps are unclear for both parties.
5. No way to re-contact a seller after leaving the market.

**User Interview – Emmanuel, 34, vegetable seller:**
> "I have many customers but they only come when they pass by. If I could tell them I have fresh matoke and avocados today, more people would come. Now I just wait. Some days I lose product because nobody knew I had it."

## Member 2 – Field Observations (Remera Neighborhood, 10:00–10:15 AM)

**Setting:** Small shops, phone credit stalls, and home-based sellers in a residential area.

**Timestamped Observations:**
- **10:02** – A woman selling second-hand clothes posts the same content to three WhatsApp groups and must reply to each person asking "how much?" and "what sizes?"
- **10:05** – A neighbor asks about a size L item. The seller disappears inside for 5 minutes searching. Customer: *"Next time I'll check before coming."*
- **10:08** – A mobile phone repair technician is walked past by a potential customer who had no idea he was there — no sign, no online presence.
- **10:11** – A woman trying to find cooking oil nearby calls four contacts before finding a shop — takes 12 minutes.
- **10:14** – A home baker and buyer spend several minutes negotiating whether to pay cash or MoMo and when.

**Friction Points:**
1. Sellers manage stock and orders across multiple platforms — exhausting and error-prone.
2. Home-based and informal sellers are invisible beyond their personal networks.
3. Buyers cannot check what is in stock without calling or visiting.
4. No standardized payment flow for informal sellers.
5. Buyers cannot gauge distance or whether the trip is worth making.

**User Interview – Chantal, 28, home seller (clothes and baked goods):**
> "WhatsApp is good but it's too much work. I post a photo and twenty people ask the same questions. I wish there was one place where my items were listed and people could just order and pay. I would sell so much more."

---

## Member 3 – Field Observations (Nyabugogo Bus Park Area, 11:00–11:15 AM)

**Setting:** High-traffic transport hub with surrounding stalls and informal traders.

**Timestamped Observations:**
- **11:01** – A trader from outside Kigali arrives with bulk maize looking for a buyer. His contact is not answering. He waits beside his sacks.
- **11:04** – Aggressive haggling session leaves a seller visibly frustrated: *"He thinks I am lying about the price. How does he know what is real?"*
- **11:07** – Two side-by-side charcoal sellers: one has a crowd, one is idle. The only difference: the busy seller has handwritten prices on cardboard.
- **11:10** – A woman asks about delivery to Kacyiru. The seller agrees but cannot confirm cost. Deal collapses.
- **11:13** – A customer returns for an item that is now sold out. Seller: *"I did not know you wanted it again, you should have told me."*

**Friction Points:**
1. No mechanism for sellers to notify repeat customers about restocked items.
2. Visible pricing dramatically increases buyer engagement — even handwritten signs work.
3. Unconfirmed delivery costs cause deals to fail at the last moment.
4. No directory for bulk or specialty traders — they are only findable by chance.
5. No transaction record for either party after a sale.

**User Interview – Jean-Pierre, 22, student and regular shopper:**
> "I know what I want to buy, I just do not know who has it at the best price near me. I end up walking a lot. If there was an app like Jumia but for Kigali's small sellers, I would use it every day."

---

## Team Synthesis – Core Problem Statement

After reviewing all three sets of independent findings, the team converged on a single recurring pattern across all locations and all interviewees:

> **Buyers in Kigali struggle to find trusted sellers with clear prices nearby, while sellers struggle to reach customers beyond their physical location without managing multiple unreliable platforms such as WhatsApp and phone calls.**

This is simultaneously a discovery problem and a trust problem. Sellers lose revenue through invisibility; buyers lose time through lack of structured information. AfriMarket addresses both sides of this gap with one unified platform.

---

## Mapping to Progressive Project Guide Constraints

| Constraint | Research Evidence |
|---|---|
| Low/intermittent internet connectivity | All sellers use WhatsApp — smartphones are common, but data is limited. The app must minimize data consumption and support offline browsing. |
| Mobile Money as primary payment method | MoMo confusion was observed at all three sites. The app must be built around mobile money flows, not card-first checkout. |
| Low digital literacy among sellers | Sellers use WhatsApp informally but cannot operate it as a business tool. Seller onboarding and product listing UI must be icon-driven with minimal typing. |
| Trust through social proof | Every buyer prioritized personal familiarity or reputation before buying. The app must surface seller ratings, reviews, and verification badges prominently. |
